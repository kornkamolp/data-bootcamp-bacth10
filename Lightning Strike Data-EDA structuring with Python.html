Introduction¶

This notebook analyzes lightning strike data from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) for the years 2016–2018. The analysis focuses on identifying patterns and trends in lightning activity through various exploratory data analysis (EDA) techniques.

The key objectives of this analysis include:

  • Identifying locations with the highest number of lightning strikes in a single day.
  • Examining locations with the most days featuring at least one lightning strike.
  • Determining if lightning activity varies by day of the week.
  • Calculating the percentage of annual lightning strikes that occur each month.

Visualizing trends using bar graphs and other data visualizations. Additionally, we will compute weekly and quarterly lightning strike totals and present them through bar charts to identify seasonal trends and patterns over time.

Conclusion¶

The exploratory data analysis (EDA) reveals a strong seasonal trend in lightning strikes. Strikes peak in Q3 (July–September) and drop significantly in Q4 (October–December), showing a consistent pattern across 2016, 2017, and 2018. This suggests a recurring influence of seasonal weather conditions on lightning activity.

A particularly notable finding is that August stands out, with more than one-third of all lightning strikes in 2018 occurring in this month. This concentration highlights the need for further analysis of weather conditions—such as temperature, humidity, and storm frequency—to better understand the underlying causes of this seasonal pattern.

Understanding these trends is crucial for disaster preparedness and risk mitigation. Authorities can use this insight to improve early warning systems, infrastructure planning, and safety measures to minimize the impact of severe weather events.
